Module 01 / 05

Phishing & Social Engineering

Phishing remains the most common initial attack vector against organizations. At AKG, over 80% of security incidents begin with a phishing email or social engineering attempt. Your ability to detect and report these attempts is the first line of defense.

What is phishing?

Phishing is a deceptive attack in which an adversary impersonates a trusted entity — a colleague, vendor, Microsoft, or even AKG's own IT department — to trick you into revealing credentials, clicking a malicious link, or transferring funds.

High Risk

Spear phishing attacks are targeted. Attackers will research your name, role, and manager from LinkedIn before crafting a convincing message. Assume personalization does not equal legitimacy.

Red flags to identify

  • Sender email domain doesn't match the organization it claims to be from
  • Urgent or threatening language: "Your account will be suspended in 2 hours"
  • Unexpected password reset, MFA prompt, or DocuSign requests
  • Links that don't match their display text (hover before clicking)
  • Requests to bypass normal process — "Don't create a ticket, just send me the file"
  • Invoices or wire transfer requests with changed banking details
  • Unsolicited attachments, even from known senders (their account may be compromised)

Real-world examples

Phishing

From: it-support@akg-helpdesk.net

"Your VPN license expired. Click here to reactivate within 24 hours or lose access."

Legitimate

From: helpdesk@akg.com

"Your VPN client needs updating. Please open the Software Center and search for 'GlobalProtect'."

Social engineering tactics

Beyond email, attackers use phone calls (vishing), SMS (smishing), and in-person pretexting. Common scenarios at corporate environments include:

  • IT impersonation: "This is IT, we need your credentials to fix an issue on your account"
  • Executive fraud (BEC): Impersonating the CEO to request urgent wire transfers
  • Vendor pretexting: Fake supplier calls requesting updated payment details
  • Tailgating requests: Unknown person asking you to hold a secure door for them
Rule

IT will never ask for your password — by phone, email, or chat. If anyone requests your credentials, refuse and report to it-security@akg.com immediately.

What to do with a suspicious email

1

Do not click any links or open attachments

Even "unsubscribe" links in suspicious emails can confirm your address is active.

2

Use the "Report Phishing" button in Outlook

This sends the email to the security team for analysis and removes it from your inbox.

3

If you clicked a link — report immediately

Don't wait. Contact IT Security at ext. 2200 or it-security@akg.com. Early reporting limits damage significantly.

Password & MFA Policy

Credential theft is responsible for the majority of unauthorized access incidents. AKG enforces a strict password and authentication policy across all systems. Compliance is mandatory and audited.

AKG password requirements

  • Minimum 14 characters for all corporate accounts
  • Must include uppercase, lowercase, digit, and special character
  • Passwords expire every 90 days — use a password manager to handle this
  • No reuse of the last 12 passwords
  • No use of your name, username, or "AKG" in any password
  • Never share passwords with colleagues — even temporarily, even for coverage
Policy

Sharing credentials is a policy violation regardless of intent. If a colleague needs access, submit an access request via the IT portal — not by sharing your password.

Use a password manager

You are expected to use KeePass (corporate-approved) or Bitwarden for managing credentials. These eliminate the need to memorize complex passwords and prevent password reuse across systems.

Weak

AKG2024! — Dictionary word + year pattern. Cracked in seconds with standard tools.

Strong

Tr!#9vKm@wqLz4 — Random, 14+ chars, no pattern. Use a password manager to store it.

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

MFA is mandatory on all AKG systems — VPN, Microsoft 365, Azure, and internal applications. MFA adds a second layer that renders stolen passwords largely useless.

  • Enroll in Microsoft Authenticator within your first day
  • Use app-based TOTP — SMS-based OTP is no longer accepted on critical systems
  • Never approve an MFA prompt you did not initiate — this is an MFA fatigue attack
  • If you receive unexpected MFA prompts, change your password and report to IT immediately
Attack Vector

MFA fatigue: Attackers send repeated push notifications hoping you'll click "Approve" to stop the noise. Always confirm the request code matches what's on screen before approving.

Privileged accounts

If your role requires admin or elevated-privilege accounts, additional requirements apply: separate credentials for privileged access, Privileged Access Workstation (PAW) usage for sensitive tasks, and quarterly access reviews.

Acceptable Use Policy

The AKG Acceptable Use Policy (AUP) defines the boundaries for using company-owned technology, networks, and data. Violations can result in disciplinary action up to and including termination and legal liability.

Company assets — what's permitted

  • Use corporate devices primarily for business purposes
  • Limited personal browsing is tolerated provided it does not impact performance or security
  • Install only pre-approved software via the Software Center — no unauthorized installations
  • Connect only to trusted networks; always use VPN when working outside AKG premises
  • Corporate email is to be used for business communications only

Prohibited activities

  • Installing unlicensed software, pirated content, or personal applications (gaming, P2P)
  • Accessing, storing, or transmitting pornographic or otherwise offensive content
  • Using company resources for personal business or side projects
  • Circumventing security controls — disabling AV, using a personal VPN, using Tor
  • Connecting unauthorized devices (personal USB drives, mobile hotspots) to the corporate network
  • Storing sensitive AKG data on personal devices, Google Drive, or unapproved cloud services
  • Using personal email accounts to send or receive work-related documents
Zero Tolerance

Disabling or attempting to bypass security controls (EDR, DLP, firewall) is a zero-tolerance violation. This applies even if the intent was productivity-related.

Data handling obligations

All AKG data must be classified and handled according to its classification level. As a new employee, assume any document you work with is at minimum Internal unless labelled otherwise.

  • Public: No restrictions — approved marketing materials, public documentation
  • Internal: AKG employees only — do not share externally without authorization
  • Confidential: Need-to-know basis — contracts, financials, HR records
  • Restricted: Requires explicit approval — PII, customer data, security configurations
Note

When in doubt about whether you're permitted to share a document, ask your manager or submit a request to it-security@akg.com before sending. It's always better to verify first.

Module 04 / 05

Incident Reporting

Early incident reporting dramatically reduces the impact of security events. The average organization that detects a breach quickly contains it in days; organizations that discover breaches late take months. You are part of AKG's detection capability.

What qualifies as a security incident?

  • Received or clicked a phishing email or suspicious link
  • Unexpected MFA prompts or account lockouts you did not trigger
  • Antivirus or EDR alert on your device
  • Unusual device behavior — high CPU, slow network, unknown processes
  • Lost or stolen laptop, phone, or smart card/access badge
  • Unauthorized person asking for system access or credentials
  • Accidental disclosure of sensitive data to the wrong recipient
Critical

Never attempt to investigate or remediate a suspected incident on your own. Doing so can destroy forensic evidence and worsen containment. Report — do not touch.

How to report an incident

1

Immediate: Call IT Security

Internal extension: 2200. For critical incidents (active breach, ransomware), call immediately — do not rely on email.

2

Email: it-security@akg.com

For non-urgent incidents. Include: what happened, when, what systems or data are involved, and any screenshots.

3

Portal: Submit a Security Incident Ticket

Go to the IT portal → Security → Report Incident. Use this for formal documentation alongside phone/email reporting.

4

Do not discuss externally

Do not discuss potential incidents with non-IT staff, clients, or on social media until authorized by IT Security or management.

No-blame culture

AKG operates a no-blame policy for security incidents reported in good faith. If you clicked a phishing link, you will not face disciplinary action for reporting it. Delays in reporting, however, can result in significantly worse outcomes — and may be treated differently.

Remember

There are no stupid incident reports. False positives are welcome — they are far better than missed detections. When in doubt, report it.

Module 05 / 05

Physical Security & Clean Desk

Digital security controls are meaningless if an unauthorized person can walk up to an unlocked workstation or pick up a confidential document left on a desk. Physical security is as important as your firewall.

Access control

  • Your access badge is personal — never lend it to anyone, including colleagues
  • Report a lost or stolen badge immediately to reception and IT Security
  • Do not allow tailgating through secured doors, even if the person looks like they belong
  • Escort all visitors at all times while inside AKG premises
  • Visitor badges must be visible — challenge any visitor without a visible badge politely
Social Engineering Risk

Tailgating is a common social engineering tactic. A well-dressed individual carrying boxes and acting confident counts on you feeling rude to challenge them. Always verify.

Clean desk policy

All employees are required to maintain a clean desk at all times when away from their workstation — even for short periods.

  • Lock your screen before leaving your desk: Windows key + L
  • Store sensitive documents in locked drawers when not in use
  • Shred documents containing sensitive, confidential, or restricted information — never bin them
  • Do not write passwords on sticky notes or leave them near your workstation
  • Clear your desk at end of day — no documents, removable media, or handwritten notes left out

Workstation security

  • Screen lock triggers automatically after 5 minutes of inactivity — do not disable this
  • Do not leave sensitive data visible on screen in open-plan or public areas (use a privacy screen)
  • Do not plug in unknown USB devices — USB drops are a real attack vector
  • Secure your laptop with a Kensington lock when working in shared or public spaces
Daily Habit

Before you leave your desk: lock your screen, file or shred documents, and do a quick visual check of your desk surface. Make it automatic.
